ComputingEd@

Welcome to ComputingEd@UW, a community of UW faculty and students passionate about 1) research about how people learn computing and 2) the practice of how people teach computing.

Our community spans faculty and students from:

  • The Paul G. Allen School of Computer Science & Engineering (CSE)
  • The Information School (iSchool)
  • The College of Education
  • The department of Human-Centered Design & Engineering (HCDE)
  • The Department of Communication

Join the computinged@uw.edu mailing list to get infrequent announcements. Join the ComputingEd@UW Slack to participate in discussion, message the community, discuss papers and conferences, and more.

How do I meet people in the community?

  • Attend the quarterly Sound CS Ed meetup, which brings together researchers, teachers, policy makers, curriculum designers, learning technology creators, and other people passionate about computing education from across Puget Sound.
  • Visit a Puget Sound CS Teachers Association meeting, which brings together CS teachers from around the region.
  • Engage in a computing education research project.
  • Engage in one of the ways to learn to teach computing above.
  • Review the many opportunities to get involved in CSE.

How do I participate in research?

There are many ways to get started:

  • Read the Computing Education Research FAQ to learn about the field.
  • Visit the CSE 590E Computer Science Education seminar. It's got a graduate level course number, but it's open to students at all levels. It often discusses computing education research papers and their application to CS teaching.
  • Visit the Code and Cognition Lab, which has several PhD students and undergraduates that conduct computing education research.
  • Reach out to any of the faculty and students in the community to chat about research opportunities.

How do I learn to teach computing?

  • Become a CSE or Informatics teaching assistant, both of which entail some training.
  • Watch the time schedule for CSE 492T Equitable and Inclusive Computer Science Pedagogy Seminar
  • Read the Cambridge Handbook of Computing Education Research, which surveys research on teaching and learning computing up to 2018, and was partially authored by UW faculty.
  • Review the general teaching resources at the UW Center for Teaching and Learning.
  • Consider taking EDUC 251 Seeking Educational Equity and Diversity, EDC&I 351 Teaching as a Profession, and E E 406 Teaching Engineering
  • Wait patiently for our university to start CS teacher education programs in the College of Education.
  • Tutor with the Center for Learning and Undergraduate Enrichment (CLUE), the Engineering Academic Center (EAC), or the Instructional Center.
  • Leverage the knowledge base articles by AccessComputing and the materials from the AccessCSforAll project, which help you ensure your teaching is accessible to people with disabilities.
